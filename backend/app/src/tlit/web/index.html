Characteristics of the scheme

  • ASCII-only - for the benefit of non-technical users, it only uses characters that are available on most Latin-based keyboards. It works on any platform without special software.
  • Readable and easier to memorize - where the lowercase and uppercase Latin letter is not enough for all the variations of corresponding sounds, then for the sake of readability this scheme breaks with the tradition of using a single character, and allows some sequences consisting of a letter plus usually one special symbol. Thus one Latin base letter can refer to more than two Arabic letters, and such sequences reflect the original sound better than some substitute sign. It also means that ASCII punctuation marks and other symbols can be preserved for their original purpose.
  • Phonetic - some Arabic letters can be written with multiple corresponding transliteration. This can express pure vowels vs. diphtongs, or differences in dialect, or the change in pronunciation of ة.
  • Unambiguous - even though some letters are expressed with more than one character, the letter boundaries are never ambiguous because the symbol part of a sequence makes no sense in itself. Combinations like th, dh, sh were avoided as they could be interpreted as t + h, etc. instead of a single letter. This is critical for computer processing. Transliterated text is convertible to Arabic script and vice versa. However, phonetic variations will be lost in the reverse conversion, and the most common transliterated version will be used.
  • Embeddable - since characters like {} and [] remain free due to sequences, they can be used for changing between Arabic and Latin context inline. {} marks the beginning and end of Arabic text within a paragraph, while [] marks numerical context within Arabic (since numbers 0, 1, 2, 3 are in use to form letters or marks).
  • Printable - removal of the diacritic marks is a simple process, so a text with diacritics is easily convertible to Unicode with or without them. When a sequence implicitly contains a tashkil, it can be undone with an * after it. Text that does not include diacritics is also prefectly valid for regular printing.

Additional notes

  • Capital I was not used, as it is not distinguishable from lowercase L in sans-serif fonts.
  • Persian support will be complete soon, others are questionable due to their special features.
  • Unsupported special characters can be included as Unicode within the transliteration
